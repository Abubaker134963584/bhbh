Overview

Morphine is an opioid analgesic drug derived from the opium poppy plant (Papaver somniferum). It is the principal alkaloid of opium and was first isolated in 1804 by Friedrich Sertürner. Morphine is considered the gold standard of analgesics for the treatment of severe pain and is named after Morpheus, the Greek god of dreams.

Key Facts

  • Schedule II controlled substance due to high potential for abuse
  • Acts primarily on μ-opioid receptors in the central nervous system
  • Available in multiple formulations including oral, parenteral, and extended-release
  • Considered the standard against which new analgesics are measured

Classification

Opioid analgesic, natural opium alkaloid

Potency

High potency, approximately 10 times stronger than codeine

Legal Status

Schedule II controlled substance (US), Class A drug (UK), Schedule I(Pakistan-DRAP rules)

Chemistry

Chemical Composition

Chemical Structure

Morphine is a benzylisoquinoline alkaloid with a chemical formula of C17H19NO3 and a molecular weight of 285.34 g/mol. Its IUPAC name is (5α,6α)-7,8-didehydro-4,5-epoxy-17-methylmorphinan-3,6-diol.

Structural Features

  • Five condensed rings including a phenol ring
  • Two hydroxyl groups (at positions 3 and 6)
  • A tertiary nitrogen atom in a piperidine ring
  • An oxygen bridge between carbons 4 and 5

Toxicology

Morphine exhibits significant toxicity, particularly at high doses or in opioid-naïve individuals. The toxic effects are primarily extensions of its pharmacological actions.

Acute Toxicity

  • LD50 (mouse, IV): 226 mg/kg
  • LD50 (rat, oral): 335 mg/kg
  • Estimated lethal dose in humans: 120-250 mg (opioid-naïve adults)

The classic triad of opioid toxicity includes:

  • Respiratory depression (most significant life-threatening effect)
  • Pinpoint pupils (miosis)
  • Decreased level of consciousness

Naloxone, a pure opioid antagonist, is the specific antidote for morphine toxicity and overdose.

Pharmacology

Mechanism of Action

Morphine primarily exerts its effects through interaction with opioid receptors, particularly the μ (mu) opioid receptors, which are G-protein coupled receptors widely distributed throughout the central nervous system and peripheral tissues.

Receptor Binding

  • μ (mu) receptors: High affinity (primary target)
  • κ (kappa) receptors: Moderate affinity
  • δ (delta) receptors: Low affinity

Cellular Effects

  • Inhibits adenylyl cyclase, reducing cAMP production
  • Activates G-protein-coupled inwardly rectifying K+ channels
  • Inhibits voltage-gated Ca2+ channels
  • Reduces neuronal excitability and neurotransmitter release

Pharmacodynamics

SystemEffects
Central Nervous System
  • Analgesia (pain relief)
  • Sedation and drowsiness
  • Euphoria
  • Respiratory depression
  • Suppression of cough reflex
  • Miosis (pupillary constriction)
  • Nausea and vomiting (via chemoreceptor trigger zone)
Gastrointestinal System
  • Decreased gastric motility
  • Delayed gastric emptying
  • Increased tone in GI smooth muscle
  • Constipation
  • Biliary tract spasm
Cardiovascular System
  • Peripheral vasodilation
  • Orthostatic hypotension
  • Histamine release (with IV administration)
  • Bradycardia (at high doses)
Urinary System
  • Increased tone of urinary tract smooth muscle
  • Urinary retention
  • Antidiuretic effect
Immune System
  • Immunosuppression
  • Histamine release

Dose-Response Relationship

  • ED50 (parenteral): 10 mg in opioid-naïve adults
  • Ceiling effect: Unlike for respiratory depression, there is no ceiling effect for analgesia
  • Tolerance develops with repeated administration

The therapeutic index (ratio of toxic to effective dose) of morphine is relatively narrow, which contributes to its potential for adverse effects and overdose.

Pharmacokinetics

Absorption

The absorption of morphine varies significantly depending on the route of administration:

RouteBioavailabilityOnset of ActionPeak Effect

Intravenous100%5-10 minutes20 minutes

Subcutaneous90-100%10-30 minutes50-90 minutes
Rectal30-40%20-60 minutes1-2 hours

Distribution

  • Volume of distribution: 3-4 L/kg
  • Plasma protein binding: 30-35% (primarily to albumin)
  • Blood-brain barrier penetration: Limited due to polarity and P-glycoprotein efflux
  • Placental transfer: Readily crosses the placenta
  • Breast milk: Excreted in small amounts

Morphine concentrates in the kidneys, liver, lungs, spleen, and skeletal muscle. Despite its effects on the CNS, only small amounts cross the blood-brain barrier due to its relatively polar nature.

The low oral bioavailability is due to significant first-pass metabolism in the liver before reaching systemic circulation.

Metabolism

Morphine undergoes extensive hepatic metabolism, primarily through glucuronidation:

Primary Metabolic Pathways
  • Glucuronidation (Phase II) by UDP-glucuronosyltransferase (UGT) enzymes:
    • Morphine-3-glucuronide (M3G): 45-55% (inactive)
    • Morphine-6-glucuronide (M6G): 10-15% (active, more potent than morphine)
  • Minor pathways:
    • N-demethylation to normorphine (5-10%)
    • Sulfation (small amounts)

The primary enzyme responsible for morphine metabolism is UGT2B7, with some contribution from UGT1A1 and UGT1A8. Genetic polymorphisms in these enzymes can affect morphine metabolism and response.

M6G is a potent μ-opioid receptor agonist with analgesic activity 2-4 times greater than morphine. It contributes significantly to the analgesic effects, especially with chronic oral administration. M3G has little to no analgesic activity but may contribute to side effects including hyperalgesia and allodynia.

Excretion

Morphine and its metabolites are primarily eliminated through the kidneys:

  • 90% of a dose is excreted in urine within 24 hours:
    • Morphine-3-glucuronide: 45-55%
    • Morphine-6-glucuronide: 10-15%
    • Free morphine: 10%
    • Other metabolites: 15-20%
  • 7-10% is excreted in feces via biliary excretion
  • Elimination half-life: 2-3 hours for morphine, 4-6 hours for M6G
  • Clearance: 15-30 mL/min/kg (reduced in renal impairment)

Special Populations

  • Renal impairment: Accumulation of M3G and M6G, requiring dose reduction
  • Hepatic impairment: Reduced clearance, increased bioavailability, requiring dose reduction
  • Elderly: Increased sensitivity, reduced clearance, requiring dose reduction
  • Neonates: Reduced glucuronidation capacity, prolonged half-life (6-7 hours)

The pharmacokinetics of morphine can be significantly altered in patients with renal impairment, as the accumulation of active metabolites (particularly M6G) can lead to prolonged and enhanced effects.

Indications and Therapeutic Uses

Morphine is primarily indicated for the management of pain, particularly moderate to severe pain that is not responsive to non-opioid analgesics. Its therapeutic applications include:

Pain Management

  • Acute pain:
    • Postoperative pain
    • Traumatic pain
    • Burns
    • Myocardial infarction
    • Renal colic
  • Chronic pain:
    • Cancer pain
    • End-of-life care
    • Selected cases of non-malignant pain

Other Indications

  • Dyspnea in palliative care
  • Acute pulmonary edema
  • Diarrhea (rarely used)
  • Cough suppression (rarely used)
  • Anesthesia (adjunct)
  • Procedural sedation
  • Opioid maintenance therapy (rarely used)

Dosing Considerations

General Dosing Guidelines (Adults)

  • Oral immediate-release: 10-30 mg every 4 hours as needed
  • Oral extended-release: 15-30 mg every 8-12 hours or 30-60 mg every 24 hours
  • Intravenous: 2.5-15 mg every 2-4 hours as needed
  • Intramuscular/Subcutaneous: 5-20 mg every 4 hours as needed
  • Rectal: 10-30 mg every 4 hours as needed
  • Epidural: 1-6 mg for single dose, 2-4 mg/day by continuous infusion
  • Intrathecal: 0.2-1 mg for single dose

Dose adjustments are necessary for elderly patients, those with renal or hepatic impairment, and patients with respiratory disease. Titration should be performed carefully to achieve adequate pain control while minimizing adverse effects.

Clinical Pharmacology

Therapeutic Principles

The clinical use of morphine is guided by several key principles to maximize efficacy while minimizing risks:

Pain Assessment

  • Comprehensive pain assessment before initiation
  • Regular reassessment of pain intensity and character
  • Use of validated pain scales (e.g., Numeric Rating Scale, Visual Analog Scale)
  • Assessment of functional impact of pain
  • Evaluation of risk factors for opioid misuse

Dosing Strategy

  • "Start low, go slow" approach, especially in opioid-naïve patients
  • Titration based on response and tolerability
  • Use of scheduled dosing for continuous pain
  • Provision of breakthrough pain medication
  • Regular reassessment of continued need
  • Consideration of opioid rotation if inadequate response or intolerable side effects

Special Clinical Considerations

Tolerance, Dependence, and Addiction

  • Tolerance: Decreased response to the same dose over time, requiring dose escalation to maintain effect
  • Physical dependence: Physiological adaptation resulting in withdrawal symptoms upon abrupt discontinuation
  • Psychological dependence/addiction: Compulsive use despite harm, characterized by craving and loss of control

Management strategies include gradual dose tapering when discontinuing, monitoring for signs of misuse, and implementing risk mitigation strategies.

Opioid-Induced Hyperalgesia

Paradoxical increase in pain sensitivity that may occur with long-term opioid use. Management may include opioid dose reduction, opioid rotation, or addition of adjuvant analgesics.

Monitoring and Follow-up

  • Pain control and functional status
  • Adverse effects (constipation, nausea, sedation, respiratory depression)
  • Signs of tolerance or hyperalgesia
  • Aberrant drug-related behaviors
  • Urine drug testing when appropriate
  • Review of prescription drug monitoring program data

Adverse Effects

Common Adverse Effects

Gastrointestinal

  • Constipation (most common, 40-95%)
  • Nausea and vomiting (15-30%)
  • Dry mouth
  • Biliary spasm
  • Anorexia

Central Nervous System

  • Sedation and drowsiness (20-60%)
  • Dizziness and lightheadedness
  • Confusion
  • Euphoria or dysphoria
  • Headache
  • Myoclonus
  • Sleep disturbances

Other

  • Pruritus (itching, 10-50%)
  • Urinary retention
  • Sweating
  • Flushing
  • Orthostatic hypotension
  • Miosis (pupillary constriction)

Serious Adverse Effects

Respiratory

  • Respiratory depression (dose-dependent)
  • Respiratory arrest
  • Bronchospasm

Cardiovascular

  • Severe hypotension
  • Bradycardia
  • Circulatory collapse
  • Cardiac arrest (rare)

Neurological

  • Seizures (rare, usually with high doses)
  • Delirium
  • Hallucinations
  • Serotonin syndrome (with concomitant serotonergic drugs)

Immunological

  • Anaphylaxis
  • Severe allergic reactions
  • Immunosuppression

Long-term Effects

Chronic use of morphine can lead to additional concerns:

  • Tolerance (decreased effect with same dose)
  • Physical dependence (withdrawal syndrome upon discontinuation)
  • Hyperalgesia (increased sensitivity to pain)
  • Endocrine effects:
    • Hypogonadism
    • Decreased testosterone levels
    • Amenorrhea
    • Reduced libido
    • Infertility
    • Immune system suppression
  • Psychological dependence/addiction (in susceptible individuals)

High-Risk Groups

  • Elderly: Increased sensitivity, altered pharmacokinetics, higher risk of falls and cognitive effects
  • Neonates and infants: Increased sensitivity to respiratory depression, immature metabolism
  • Patients with renal impairment: Accumulation of active metabolites (M6G)
  • Patients with hepatic impairment: Reduced clearance, increased bioavailability
  • Patients with respiratory disorders: COPD, sleep apnea, asthma - increased risk of respiratory depression
  • Patients with head injury or increased ICP: Risk of further increases in intracranial pressure
  • Patients with history of substance use disorder: Increased risk of misuse and addiction

Drug Interactions

Morphine interacts with numerous medications, potentially leading to altered efficacy or increased toxicity. Understanding these interactions is crucial for safe prescribing.

Pharmacodynamic Interactions

CNS Depressants (High Risk)

  • Concurrent use increases risk of sedation, respiratory depression, hypotension, and death:
    • Other opioids
    • Benzodiazepines and other sedative-hypnotics
    • General anesthetics
    • Alcohol
    • Barbiturates
    • Antipsychotics
    • Skeletal muscle relaxants
    • Gabapentinoids (gabapentin, pregabalin)

Serotonergic Drugs

May increase risk of serotonin syndrome:

  • Selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors (SSRIs)
  • Serotonin-norepinephrine reuptake inhibitors (SNRIs)
  • Tricyclic antidepressants
  • Monoamine oxidase inhibitors (MAOIs)
  • Triptans
  • Linezolid
  • Lithium

Anticholinergic Drugs

May enhance anticholinergic effects:

  • Antihistamines
  • Tricyclic antidepressants
  • Antipsychotics
  • Antispasmodics

Increased risk of constipation, urinary retention, confusion, and cognitive impairment.

Pharmacokinetic Interactions

UGT Enzyme Inhibitors

  • May increase morphine levels by inhibiting glucuronidation:
    • Valproic acid
    • Probenecid
    • Some NSAIDs
    • Atazanavir

UGT Enzyme Inducers

  • May decrease morphine levels by enhancing glucuronidation:
    • Rifampin
    • Carbamazepine
    • Phenytoin
    • St. John's Wort

P-glycoprotein Modulators

P-glycoprotein affects morphine transport across the blood-brain barrier and intestinal absorption:

  • Inhibitors (may increase CNS effects): quinidine, verapamil, cyclosporine, ketoconazole
  • Inducers (may decrease effects): rifampin, St. John's Wort

Specific Clinically Significant Interactions

Drug/Drug ClassInteractionClinical Management
Benzodiazepines Synergistic CNS and respiratory depression; increased mortality risk Avoid combination when possible; if necessary, use lowest effective doses with close monitoring
Monoamine Oxidase Inhibitors (MAOIs) Unpredictable, potentially fatal reactions including excitation, hyperpyrexia, convulsions, or respiratory depression Avoid combination; wait at least 14 days after discontinuing MAOIs before starting morphine
Mixed Agonist/Antagonist Opioids (e.g., pentazocine, nalbuphine) May reduce analgesic effect of morphine and precipitate withdrawal in dependent patients Avoid combination
Cimetidine May increase morphine effects by reducing first-pass metabolism Monitor for increased effects; consider dose reduction
Diuretics Morphine may reduce diuretic efficacy by inducing ADH release Monitor fluid balance and diuretic response
Antihypertensives Enhanced hypotensive effects Monitor blood pressure; consider dose adjustments

Food Interactions

Food interactions with morphine are generally minimal but may include:

  • High-fat meals may slightly increase the absorption of oral morphine
  • Grapefruit juice may theoretically increase morphine bioavailability by inhibiting intestinal CYP3A4, though this is of limited clinical significance
  • Alcohol significantly enhances CNS and respiratory depression and should be avoided

Monitoring Recommendations

  • Perform comprehensive medication review before initiating morphine
  • Consider potential interactions when adding new medications to a regimen that includes morphine
  • Monitor more frequently when starting, stopping, or changing doses of interacting medications
  • Educate patients about potential interactions, particularly with alcohol and over-the-counter medications
  • Consider dose adjustments or alternative analgesics when significant interactions cannot be avoided
